# Tips & Tricks

- **Map RATE to a macro knob** — RATE is the most important control. When performing, keep it accessible. Slow sweeps from 7 o'clock to 3 o'clock take you from sparse generative CV to full digital noise in one gesture.
- **Find your X/Y islands** — not all X/Y combinations are equal. Some produce long, non-repeating sequences; others loop quickly. Spend time mapping combinations that work for your style and note the X/Y positions on the panel with wax pencil or tape.
- **S as a "favourites" selector** — once you've found a musical stretch of a sequence, set S to the start of that region. S TRIG then becomes a "jump to best part" button during performance.
- **Mode 3 is most musical** — ZX mode produces the most tonal, pitched output. It's paradoxically the most "musical" setting despite being named after a data encoding standard. Start here if you want chiptune-adjacent results.
- **CV over Y for pitch-like control** — in Mode 3 at mid-high RATE, CV into Y input creates changes in the tonal character that resemble pitch modulation. Patch a V/oct sequence in for something approaching melodic control over the noise character.
- **S TRIG with audio** — patching a drum hit or other audio transient into S TRIG creates unpredictable, sample-accurate triggering of sequence resets. The results are rhythmically related to the audio but not cleanly quantized — useful for glitchy, reactive patches.
- **Pair with Black CS Expander for percussion** — the Expander's autobend adds an attack envelope to the internal clock pitch, enabling the Code Source to generate complete chiptune percussion hits (attack transient + decaying tone) without external envelope modules.
- **Output polarity matters** — in bipolar mode, negative voltages can close VCAs and reverse attenuverters. If you're getting unexpected silence or reversed behavior, consider switching to unipolar via the PCB jumper, or running outputs through an offset/rectifier.
