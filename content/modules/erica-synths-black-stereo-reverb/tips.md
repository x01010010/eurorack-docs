# Tips

## Dirty BBD for Drums

The dirty BBD source adds subtle noise and non-linearity that glues well with percussion. Use it with Room mode and a short SIZE setting for a gritty, characterful ambience that doesn't smear transients.

## Save Matched Preset Pairs

Save a dry preset (DRY/WET low, Room mode) and a wet preset (DRY/WET high, Cathedral mode) in adjacent slots. Use the PRESET CV input to crossfade between them during a performance — effectively automating a dramatic build.

## Tone CV for Darker Tails

As a reverb decays, the high frequencies often feel harsh on long tails. Patch a slow falling envelope to TONE CV so that as the tail plays out, it progressively darkens. This mimics natural acoustic absorption and produces a more realistic decay character.

## Freeze + SIZE CV for Living Pads

While Freeze is held, modulate SIZE CV with a slow LFO. The frozen buffer responds to size changes — shifting the pitch and texture of the held tone slightly. This gives the frozen pad a subtle, organic movement without releasing it.

## Use IN R for True Stereo Processing

When processing stereo sources (e.g., a stereo oscillator, a panned mix bus), patch both channels to IN L and IN R. The module maintains true stereo separation through the reverb algorithm, preserving the spatial relationship of the source.

## Preset Slots for Different Instruments

Dedicate specific preset slots to recurring patch roles: one for drums (short, room), one for leads (medium hall, moderate feedback), one for pads (cathedral, long decay, high spin). Switching between them with PRESET CV during a set keeps the reverb consistently matched to the active voice.

## 112dB Algorithm Quality

The reverb algorithms were developed by 112dB, a Dutch company known for high-end plug-ins and DSP work. The result is noticeably high-fidelity — low noise floor, natural tails, and absence of metallic artefacts common in budget digital reverbs.
