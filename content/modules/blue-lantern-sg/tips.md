# Tips

**Start with square wave input.** The CD4013 flip-flop divides the signal by detecting zero-crossings. A square wave has the clearest, fastest zero-crossings, giving the cleanest division. Saw waves work well too and produce richer harmonic content in the sub layers. Triangle and sine waves are also fully usable.

**Use Knob 3 as your foundation.** The one-octave-below division (Knob 3) is the most musically neutral sub-harmonic — same note, same tonality, just lower. Start with this knob when building a sub bass layer, then add others to taste.

**Knob 4 for sub-bass rumble.** Two octaves down pushes into genuine sub-bass territory. Use it to add weight that is felt as much as heard, especially in a live PA context. At moderate levels it rounds out the low end; at high levels it dominates.

**Keep Knobs 1 and 2 subtle.** The 7th-interval divisions are harmonically unusual — not wrong, but they don't disappear into the background. Use them at low to medium levels as a flavoring rather than a dominant layer. They are most effective in dense textures, drones, and pad-like contexts rather than melodic bass lines.

**Always pair with a mixer.** **SG** outputs the sub mix only; the original VCO signal is not passed through. Keep the dry VCO output patched separately to your mixer alongside the SG output. The balance between original and sub determines how thick vs. how deep the final sound feels.

**Filter the sub mix separately.** Because the SG output is a separate signal path, you can route it through its own low-pass filter before the mixer. Cutting the highs from the sub mix while keeping the full-spectrum original VCO gives a cleaner, more controlled bass texture.

**Tune the VCO precisely before using SG.** Since SG divides the input frequency, any pitch drift in the VCO is also divided and appears in the sub layers. A well-tuned, thermally stable VCO sounds best with sub-harmonic division.

**Experiment with slow-LFO input.** At very low input rates the output approaches sub-audio pulsing rather than pitched tones. Feeding a slow LFO or audio-rate LFO into the IN jack at low frequencies can produce interesting rhythmic subdivision effects.
