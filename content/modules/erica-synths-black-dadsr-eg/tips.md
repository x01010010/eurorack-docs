# Tips

## The Delay Stage Changes the Rhythmic Feel

Even a small gate delay (50–100 ms) shifts the perceptual onset of a modulation event in a way that a slow Attack cannot — the note or sound starts immediately but the modulation follows, which mimics the natural behaviour of many acoustic instruments where pitch, timbre, and amplitude evolve at different rates after the initial transient.

## Use DELAYED GATE OUT Even If the Envelope Is Already in Use

The DELAYED GATE OUT is available at all times. Patch it to a second module (another envelope, a sample & hold, a sequencer advance) to create offset events driven by the same timing source as the primary envelope.

## CV Over Decay Is the Most Expressive Control

For most tonal and percussive sounds, Decay time has the greatest impact on perceived energy and dynamics. Patching a velocity CV (from a keyboard, pressure, or a track on a sequencer) to the Decay CV input with the attenuator set conservatively gives dynamic response where harder hits have longer, more sustaining envelopes.

## Looping Mode + CV = Complex LFO

In loop mode, independently modulating Attack and Release with different LFOs creates an envelope shape that continuously changes its rise and fall times. The result is an irregular, non-repeating modulation pattern that feels organic rather than mechanical — useful for pads, drones, or generative patches.

## Maximum CV Extends Times Significantly

The CV inputs can extend stage times well beyond the knob maximum — Attack from 2.5 s to 4.4 s, Decay to 12 s, Release to 10 s. For very slow evolving textures, set the knob to maximum and send a positive voltage to the CV input to reach the extended range.

## Re-Triggering for Rhythmic Texture

Sending a fast clock or LFO square wave to GATE IN with a moderate Attack and short Decay creates a machine-gun retrigger effect where the envelope never fully completes — it restarts on each hit, maintaining the Attack-into-Decay peak and adding rhythmic amplitude density. Useful for sequenced filter effects on sustained sounds.

## Manual Gate for Live Performance

The GATE button is a useful hands-on tool in a live context — hold it down to sustain a pad or texture, release to let it decay. Pair with the DELAY knob to add a slight onset lag for more expressive manual control.
