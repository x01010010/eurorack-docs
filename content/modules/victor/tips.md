# Tips & Tricks

- **Corner assignment determines timbral journey** — the character of vector sweeps depends entirely on what waveforms you assign to each corner. Put contrasting waveforms at diagonal corners (A opposite D, B opposite C) for the widest timbral range. Put similar waveforms on one axis for gradual evolution.
- **Slow envelope sweep ≠ LFO sweep** — use an envelope for a per-note timbral arc (each note has the same shape); use an LFO for continuous evolution independent of individual notes. Both are valid — choose based on whether timbre should be note-locked or free.
- **X and Y outputs for spectral splits** — X carries the blend of the two X-axis extremes (A+B vs C+D); Y carries the Y-axis blend. These are harmonically different from MIX. Route them to different effects for a complex stereo field from one oscillator.
- **128 waveforms is too many to explore randomly** — make a map. Assign each corner a category (sustain waves, attack transients, harmonic-rich, noise-like) and explore within categories. The OLED display names each waveform to help.
- **Very shallow depth = subtle timbre variation** — small X/Y CV ranges (±1V around centre) keep vector movement subtle and musical. Push to full range (±5V) only when you want dramatic, obvious timbre shifts.
- **FM at audio rate** — patching audio-rate FM creates complex inharmonic tones on top of the vector blend. Moderate FM depth with a slow vector sweep creates a rich, animated FM-vector hybrid texture.
