# Tips

- **DRIVE before the filter, not after.** The overdrive circuit feeds into the VCF input. Higher DRIVE settings thicken and saturate the sound before filtering — stack with resonance for aggressive leads.

- **Paraphonic works best with slow attacks.** Because both voices share a single envelope, fast attacks create a slight timing ambiguity. Slower attacks mask this and produce lush, evolving two-voice chords.

- **The VCA LEVEL knob opens the VCA without a gate.** Useful for drone patches: set LEVEL above zero, play a pitch, and the sound sustains indefinitely without needing a gate signal.

- **Run the BBD slow for slapback, fast for chorus.** At longer DELAY TIME values it's a traditional slapback; at very short times with moderate FEEDBACK it thickens the sound like a chorus/flanger.

- **Self-oscillating VCF tracks pitch reasonably well.** Patch 1 V/oct to VCF CUTOFF — it won't be as accurate as a dedicated VCO but works for bass drones and effect layers.

- **Use the S&H to randomise filter, not just pitch.** Patch S&H OUT to VCF CUTOFF with an attenuator in between. Each clock pulse opens the filter to a new random depth — great for evolving sequences.

- **750 mA +12 V is significant.** This is one of the highest draws in the Behringer semi-modular range. If housing it in a Eurorack case alongside other modules, verify your power supply has the headroom.

- **The summers and multiples are genuinely useful for external patching.** Even if you rarely use the Neutron's own voice, the utility section (attenuators, summers, mult, slew) works on any Eurorack signal and justifies the panel real estate.
