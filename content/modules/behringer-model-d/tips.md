# Tips

- **Detune OSC 2 and 3 slightly.** Even a few cents of detune between all three oscillators creates the thick, chorusing unison sound the Minimoog is famous for. TUNE knobs on OSC 2 and 3 are your main tools here.

- **OSC 3 free mode is the classic trick.** Disengage KB CTRL 3 and set OSC 3 to a low RANGE. Blend it lightly in the mixer for a subtle pitch flutter, or push it harder and route it to the VCF for filter vibrato. This is how most vintage Minimoog users got movement without an external LFO.

- **The DECAY/RELEASE switch matters.** In the down position, DECAY time and RELEASE time are independent (longer, more controllable). In the up position, DECAY also controls RELEASE. For plucky sounds, keep it up; for sustained pads where you want a long release without a long decay, switch it down.

- **Use CONTOUR (filter env depth) subtly for leads.** A touch of filter envelope adds attack transient snap to a lead patch without a full filter sweep. CONTOUR at 20–30% with a fast attack shapes the top end without overwhelming the note.

- **Power draw is heavy.** At 583 mA on +12V, the Model D alone consumes a large portion of most small case supplies. Budget at least 800–900 mA headroom on the +12V rail if housing it alongside other modules.

- **The ladder filter self-oscillates cleanly.** At maximum EMPHASIS with CONTOUR at zero, the filter produces a stable sine-like tone. Track it with the 1V/oct jack if available for a fourth "oscillator."

- **Poly chain requires identical patch settings.** When chaining multiple Model D units, all need the same oscillator, filter, and envelope settings to sound uniform. Treat them as a keyboard instrument, not as independently patchable voices.

- **Calibration may drift with temperature.** Like many analog synths, the VCOs can drift during warm-up. Allow a few minutes before critical tuning work, especially in cold environments.
