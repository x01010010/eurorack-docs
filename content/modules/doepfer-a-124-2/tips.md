# Tips

## Embrace the Grit

The CD4069 circuit is the entire point. Do not try to make it clean — the slight harshness at high resonance and warm saturation at high input levels are the Wasp's signature. Use it on sources where a polished, clinical filter would sound sterile.

## Trimmer Controls

The Mix and Input Level controls are small trimmers without knobs — they are best set during patching and left alone rather than automated or swept. For automated Mix morphing, use a CV into CV2 instead.

## Third Output Jumper

Decide at installation whether you want LP or HP on the third output. A common choice is HP, since the MIX OUT naturally defaults toward LP and already gives easy LP access at the MIX knob's minimum. Having an independent HP jack lets you run dual paths without using the MIX knob.

## Notch as Band-Pass Complement

The notch response at center MIX is the inverse of a bandpass — it passes everything except a narrow band. Sweeping FREQ while in notch mode is a classic guitar-wah style effect, adding a hollow or "telephony" quality to percussive or melodic material.

## CD4069 Longevity

The CD4069 chip is known to occasionally fail when stressed over many years (it is being operated outside its datasheet specifications). If the filter stops working, the chip can be replaced cheaply — it is socketed for this reason. Keep a spare.

## Resonance as Texture Not Pitch

Because the filter does not self-oscillate, resonance functions only as a spectral emphasis and harmonic generator, not as a pitched sine oscillator. This makes it predictable in a mix — you can push resonance hard for character without worrying about unwanted pitched ringing.

## Series with Another Filter

Place **A-124-2** before a resonant self-oscillating filter (e.g. A-120) to use it as an input saturator and pre-filter shaper. The grit from the Wasp adds even-harmonic content that sounds good going into a smooth Moog-style lowpass.
